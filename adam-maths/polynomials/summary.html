1. Vocabulary & Basics

What is a Polynomial?

A polynomial is an expression that combines numbers, variables, and operation symbols (addition, subtraction, and multiplication). The variables have non-negative integer exponents.

Coefficient
The number multiplying a variable. In 3x², the coefficient is 3.
Leading Coefficient
The coefficient of the term with the highest exponent. In -2x³ + 5x + 1, it's -2.
Constant Term
The number with no variable attached. In x² + 4x + 7, it's 7.
Polynomial Function
A function written as f(x) = aₙxⁿ + ... + a₁x + a₀ where a's are coefficients and n is a non-negative integer.

Standard Form vs. Factored Form

Standard Form

Written as powers in descending order:

Example
f(x) = 2x³ - 5x² + 3x + 1

Use for: Finding the degree, identifying the leading coefficient, end behavior.

Factored Form

Written as a product of factors:

Example
f(x) = 2(x - 1)(x + 3)²

Use for: Finding zeros/roots, determining multiplicities, sketching graphs.

2. Roots, Zeros & X-Intercepts

These three terms mean the same thing—the x-values where the graph touches or crosses the x-axis.
Root
The solution(s) when you set a polynomial equation equal to zero and solve. For f(x) = x² - 3x + 2, set 0 = (x - 1)(x - 2), so the roots are x = 1 and x = 2.
Zero
The x-value(s) that make the function equal zero. Same as roots, but the language used when discussing functions.
X-Intercept
The point(s) where the graph crosses the x-axis. If a zero is x = 1, the x-intercept is the point (1, 0).

How to Find Zeros

  1. Use factored form — set each factor equal to zero: (x - 1)(x + 2) = 0 → x = 1 or x = -2
  2. Factor first — if given standard form, factor before solving
  3. Read the graph — identify where the graph touches or crosses the x-axis

Y-Intercept

The y-intercept is where the graph crosses the y-axis. To find it: plug in x = 0 and solve for y.

Example
f(x) = 2x² - 3x + 5 f(0) = 2(0)² - 3(0) + 5 = 5 Y-intercept: (0, 5)

3. Degree & Leading Coefficient

Degree of a Polynomial

The degree is the highest exponent in the polynomial.

Standard Form
Example
f(x) = 3x⁴ - 2x² + x + 7 Degree = 4
Factored Form
Example
f(x) = 2(x-1)³(x+2) Degree = 3 + 1 = 4

What Does Degree Tell Us?

  • Maximum number of x-intercepts: A polynomial of degree n can have at most n real zeros.
  • Shape of the graph: Higher degrees = more "wiggles"
  • End behavior: Depends on degree + sign of leading coefficient (see below)

Leading Coefficient

The coefficient of the term with the highest power. Its sign (positive or negative) matters for end behavior.

🔍 Quick Check

In any form, find the term with the highest power—that coefficient is your leading coefficient.

4. Multiplicity & Graph Behavior

What is Multiplicity?

When a zero (root) appears more than once—meaning the same factor appears multiple times in the factored form.

Example
f(x) = (x - 3)²(x + 1) The factor (x - 3) appears twice. Zero at x = 3 has multiplicity 2 Zero at x = -1 has multiplicity 1

Behavior at X-Intercepts (Critical!)

Bounce (Even Multiplicity)
If a factor appears an even number of times, the graph "bounces" or "touches" the x-axis without crossing. The graph stays on the same side before and after.
Cross (Multiplicity = 1)
If a factor appears once, the graph crosses the x-axis linearly, like a straight line would. This is the "normal" crossing behavior.
Wiggle (Odd Multiplicity > 1)
If a factor appears an odd number of times greater than 1 (like 3, 5, etc.), the graph "wiggles" or "slides" through the x-axis with a slight curve/inflection at the zero.
Visual Summary
Multiplicity 1: | Crosses (linear) Multiplicity 2: ⌢ Bounces (parabola-like) Multiplicity 3: S Wiggles (cubic curve) Multiplicity 4: ⌢ Bounces Multiplicity 5: S Wiggles ...and so on
⚠️ Remember

Even multiplicities = bounce. Odd multiplicities = cross or wiggle depending on whether it's 1 or >1.

5. End Behavior

End behavior describes what happens to the graph as x → ∞ (far right) and x → -∞ (far left).

The Four Patterns

Even Degree + Positive Leading Coefficient
Both ends go UP: ⌢ (like an upside-down U or a valley when inverted)
Even Degree + Negative Leading Coefficient
Both ends go DOWN: ⌢ (like an upside-down U, inverted)
Odd Degree + Positive Leading Coefficient
Left end goes DOWN, right end goes UP: (like a general increasing line)
Odd Degree + Negative Leading Coefficient
Left end goes UP, right end goes DOWN: (like a general decreasing line)

Quick Decision Tree

Step 1: Is the degree even or odd?
Step 2: Is the leading coefficient positive or negative?
Step 3: Use the four patterns above.
Example
f(x) = -3x⁴ + 2x² - 1 Degree: 4 (even) Leading coefficient: -3 (negative) End behavior: Both ends go DOWN

6. Graphing Strategy

To sketch a polynomial graph, follow these steps in order:

  1. Find the x-intercepts (zeros) — Factor and solve f(x) = 0
  2. Determine multiplicity for each zero — Count how many times each factor appears
  3. Identify behavior at each intercept — Bounce, cross, or wiggle
  4. Find the y-intercept — Plug in x = 0
  5. Determine the degree — Count exponents (standard form) or add exponents in factors (factored form)
  6. Identify the leading coefficient sign — Positive or negative?
  7. Sketch end behavior — Use the four patterns above
  8. Connect the points — Draw smoothly, respecting bounces, crosses, and wiggles
Pro Tip: Factored Form is Your Friend

Always convert to or work with factored form when graphing. It tells you everything: zeros, multiplicities, and you can find the degree instantly.

7. Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Every polynomial of degree n has exactly n roots (including repeated roots and imaginary roots).

What This Means

  • A degree 3 polynomial must have exactly 3 roots (counting multiplicity)
  • A degree 5 polynomial must have exactly 5 roots
  • Some of these roots may be imaginary (complex numbers) — they won't show as x-intercepts on a real graph
  • Some roots may be repeated (multiplicity > 1)
Example
f(x) = x³ - 2x² + x This has degree 3, so it must have 3 roots (counting multiplicity). f(x) = x(x - 1)² Roots: x = 0 (multiplicity 1), x = 1 (multiplicity 2) Total: 1 + 2 = 3 roots ✓

If You Can't Find Enough Real Roots...

Don't panic. The "missing" roots are imaginary. The theorem still holds—they just don't appear on the graph.

8. Before You Solve a Problem

Polynomial Mastery Checklist

Do I know what form the polynomial is in (standard or factored)?
Have I found all zeros/roots and their multiplicities?
Can I identify bounce, cross, and wiggle at each intercept?
Do I know the degree of the polynomial?
Is the leading coefficient positive or negative?
Can I describe the end behavior correctly?
Have I found the y-intercept?
Does my sketch match all the properties above?

Quick Reference: Problem Types

Finding zeros: Factor and solve, or read from a graph

Graphing: Follow the 8-step strategy above

Analyzing behavior: Use multiplicity to determine bounce/cross/wiggle

Finding degree: Highest exponent (standard form) or sum of exponents in factors (factored form)
